=== Monthly Summaries ===

## July 2020
In July 2020, the FBI issued urgent warnings about escalating cyber threats, particularly in the healthcare, finance, and local government sectors. A significant threat emerged from malware known as GoldenSpy, embedded in Chinese government-mandated tax software, which allowed remote access for data exfiltration and execution of malicious code. This incident underscores the dangers associated with third-party software reliance.

Moreover, there was a notable rise in COVID-19-themed phishing attacks targeting user credentials, as cybercriminals exploited pandemic-related fears to execute sophisticated email phishing campaigns. The trend of Business Email Compromise (BEC) scams also grew, disproportionately affecting small and medium-sized organizations and resulting in substantial financial losses. These trends emphasize the urgent need for improved cybersecurity protocols and increased vigilance among organizations.

## September 2020
In September 2020, the financial sector experienced a significant rise in cybersecurity threats, particularly credential stuffing attacks, which accounted for 41 percent of reported incidents from 2017 to 2019. Cybercriminals leveraged botnets to automate login attempts using stolen credentials, leading to widespread account compromises and subsequent identity fraud, unauthorized transactions, and loyalty program abuse. Ransomware incidents, notably those involving the Netwalker strain, exploited the COVID-19 pandemic through phishing tactics, highlighting the urgent need for organizations to enhance their cybersecurity protocols and report incidents to authorities like the FBI.

Emerging threats during this period included advanced malware campaigns from groups such as Rana Intelligence Computing, which primarily targeted U.S. travel industry companies through social engineering tactics. In response to these escalating threats, the FBI advised organizations to adopt preventive measures, such as updating anti-virus software, implementing application control, and utilizing threat reputation services to better protect against these ongoing cyber risks.

## October 2020
In October 2020, the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) and the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) issued a crucial advisory concerning Iranian advanced persistent threat (APT) actors attempting to interfere with the U.S. Presidential Election. These actors are employing various tactics, such as creating fictitious media sites, spoofing legitimate ones, and executing distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attacks, to spread misinformation and undermine public confidence in the electoral process. 

The advisory underscores the need for immediate action to address vulnerabilities, as APT actors exploit weaknesses in web applications and utilize sophisticated evasion techniques. To combat these threats, the FBI and CISA recommend organizations strengthen their security protocols, including timely patching of vulnerabilities and educating users on identifying phishing attempts, particularly during this critical election period.

## November 2020
In November 2020, the FBI reported a concerning rise in cyber threats, particularly targeting the U.S. Census Bureau through the registration of spoofed domains aimed at malicious activities. These domains, often created via typosquatting, are being used by cybercriminals to spread malware, resulting in potential financial losses and the compromise of sensitive information. 

The healthcare sector is also increasingly vulnerable, facing advanced ransomware attacks utilizing malware such as TrickBot and BazarLoader to disrupt services and steal data. The emergence of a persistent threat identified as anchor_dns, which employs sophisticated techniques to remain undetected on infected systems, highlights the evolving landscape of cyber threats. This malware's ability to execute commands periodically while leveraging legitimate domains for stealth emphasizes the necessity for enhanced security measures, including regular audits and robust patch management, to protect against these sophisticated attacks.

## December 2020
In December 2020, cyber threat activity was heavily marked by the exploitation of vulnerabilities in SolarWinds Orion products by advanced persistent threat actors using the SUNBURST malware. This incident raised alarms across various sectors, prompting cybersecurity professionals to evaluate potential system compromises. The FBI emphasized the necessity of vigilance and swift action in mitigating these threats, particularly as ransomware attacks, especially those involving the DoppelPaymer variant, continued to disrupt critical services in healthcare and education. 

The month revealed a concerning trend of low-effort, high-reward tactics employed by cybercriminals, who increasingly targeted outdated software vulnerabilities to steal sensitive information and infiltrate networks. In response, the FBI, CISA, and MS-ISAC urged organizations, particularly educational institutions, to enhance their cybersecurity measures. The situation underscored the pressing need for organizations to adopt proactive security strategies, including timely vulnerability patching and blocking malicious websites and email attachments, to defend against the evolving landscape of cyber threats.

## January 2021
In January 2021, vishing attacks emerged as a notable threat, specifically targeting users of VoIP platforms. Cybercriminals employed these voice phishing tactics to manipulate employees into providing their usernames and passwords through deceptive phone calls. Alongside this trend, Egregor ransomware continued to gain traction, with over 150 global victims reported since its emergence in September 2020. Operating under a Ransomware as a Service model, Egregor employs various infiltration tactics, including phishing emails and the exploitation of personal accounts. Following successful breaches, attackers leverage tools such as Cobalt Strike and Qakbot to escalate their access and exfiltrate sensitive information, ultimately demanding ransom for file recovery. The FBI has highlighted the critical importance of reporting these incidents to enhance tracking and mitigation efforts against such threats.

## February 2021
In February 2021, the threat landscape revealed a concerning rise in Telephony Denial of Service (TDoS) attacks, driven by diverse motives including hacktivism, financial gain, and personal vendettas. These attacks pose serious risks to public safety by obstructing access to emergency services, particularly when they coincide with physical threats. Additionally, cybercriminals have increasingly targeted TeamViewer, a popular remote desktop sharing software, for social engineering and phishing campaigns, exploiting vulnerabilities in security protocols to gain unauthorized access to systems, similar to the impacts of Remote Access Trojans (RATs).

## March 2021
In March 2021, the cybersecurity landscape was notably impacted by the surge in Mamba and PYSA ransomware attacks, affecting various sectors, including local governments, educational institutions, and private enterprises. Mamba ransomware employs DiskCryptor to encrypt entire drives, locking users out of their systems until a ransom is paid, while PYSA ransomware increasingly targets educational institutions, using sophisticated techniques to bypass antivirus protections and exfiltrate sensitive information.

Additionally, Business Email Compromise (BEC) tactics continued to evolve, with cybercriminals employing spoofed emails and phishing attacks to manipulate financial transactions within state, local, tribal, and territorial government entities. The FBI highlighted the necessity of training employees on cybersecurity best practices and recommended implementing anti-malware solutions and awareness campaigns to address these ongoing threats effectively.

## April 2021
In April 2021, the FBI and the Department of Homeland Security warned about the intensified cyber threats from the Russian Foreign Intelligence Service (SVR), also identified as Advanced Persistent Threat 29 (APT 29). Notably, the SVR has shifted its focus from traditional malware to targeting cloud resources, especially email systems, and exploiting zero-day vulnerabilities in Management Software Systems (MES). This approach allows them to access sensitive information and credentials while remaining undetected within compromised networks.

The FBI specifically highlighted the use of WELLMESS malware linked to APT 29 in various cyber intrusions. They stressed the urgency of addressing these risks through immediate patching of vulnerabilities and malware removal, alongside continuous training for organizations on information security practices, particularly in recognizing phishing attempts.

## May 2021
In May 2021, the FBI issued alerts regarding the persistent threat from Advanced Persistent Threat (APT) actors, particularly those exploiting vulnerabilities in Fortinet products and employing techniques like spear-phishing to deploy malware such as TrickBot and Conti ransomware. A troubling increase in ransomware attacks has been observed, especially targeting critical sectors like healthcare and first responder networks, with the Conti group linked to at least 16 incidents. 

The FBI highlighted a spear-phishing campaign that impersonated a U.S.-based financial institution to distribute malicious applications, emphasizing the need for organizations to enhance their cybersecurity measures. This includes regular antivirus updates, user training, and secure backups to safeguard sensitive data. The FBI continues to advise against paying ransoms, as this may incentivize further criminal activity and undermine future security efforts.

## July 2021
In July 2021, there was a notable rise in ransomware attacks that exploited known vulnerabilities, particularly through malware campaigns targeting Pulse Secure VPN and MobileIron systems. The REvil/Sodinokibi and Cring ransomware groups exemplified this trend by chaining multiple vulnerabilities, such as CVE-2020-15505 and CVE-2020-1472, to facilitate lateral movement within compromised networks.

Furthermore, the FBI issued warnings about potential cyber disruptions during the Tokyo 2020 Summer Olympics, emphasizing the heightened risk of DDoS attacks, ransomware incidents, and phishing campaigns aimed at sensitive data theft and disruption of broadcasts. This surge in targeting high-profile events underscores the necessity for organizations to strengthen their cybersecurity measures and remain vigilant against these evolving threats.

## August 2021
In August 2021, ransomware incidents surged, with cybercriminals increasingly exploiting phishing schemes and unsecured Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP) endpoints. The DarkSide group exemplified a dangerous trend of dual extortion, encrypting data while also threatening to publish sensitive information unless ransom demands were met. Noteworthy ransomware variants like Sodinokibi/REvil and Hive emerged, focusing on data exfiltration and leveraging public disclosure pressures on victims. 

In addition, the distribution of malicious zip files containing compromised Microsoft Office documents became a prevalent tactic, facilitating the deployment of the IcedID banking Trojan. The FBI and CISA underscored the necessity for organizations to adopt proactive cybersecurity measures, such as user training and robust incident response plans, particularly during vulnerable periods like holidays and weekends. This month highlighted the pressing need for vigilance against sophisticated malware delivery methods and the importance of timely reporting of incidents.

## February 2023
In February 2023, ransomware attacks, particularly those linked to the Democratic People’s Republic of Korea (DPRK), emerged as a significant threat, especially within the healthcare and public health sectors. === Yearly Summaries ===

## 2020
In 2020, the cyber threat landscape was characterized by a significant rise in sophisticated attacks, particularly the exploitation of vulnerabilities in SolarWinds Orion products by Advanced Persistent Threat (APT) actors. The emergence of SUNBURST malware underscored the urgent need for organizations to enhance their cybersecurity defenses. Ransomware incidents surged, notably targeting critical sectors such as healthcare and education, where attackers employed aggressive extortion tactics. The shift to distance learning amid the COVID-19 pandemic provided cybercriminals with new opportunities to exploit vulnerabilities, prompting calls for robust cybersecurity practices and incident response strategies.

Additionally, the year saw a marked increase in phishing campaigns and attacks utilizing advanced malware like TrickBot and BazarLoader, often leveraging social engineering tactics to deceive users. The FBI and the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) issued warnings about the risks posed by state-sponsored groups aiming to disrupt U.S. operations, including the electoral process. Overall, 2020 highlighted the necessity for organizations to implement stringent patch management protocols, conduct regular security audits, and foster user education to effectively combat the evolving cyber threat landscape.

## 2021
In 2021, the cyber threat landscape was dominated by the exploitation of vulnerabilities in IT and cloud infrastructures, notably the Log4j and Zoho ManageEngine ServiceDesk Plus, which were targeted by advanced persistent threat (APT) groups for ransomware deployment and data exfiltration. Ransomware attacks surged dramatically, with adversaries employing sophisticated techniques such as double extortion, where sensitive data was threatened with public release unless ransom demands were met. Critical infrastructure sectors, including water and agriculture, were particularly affected, highlighting the urgent need for organizations to adopt robust cybersecurity measures, maintain thorough patch management, and implement comprehensive incident response strategies.

The year also saw a notable rise in social engineering tactics, especially spear-phishing, alongside increasing incidents of business email compromise targeting government entities. Cybercriminals leveraged widely available phishing kits, making sophisticated attacks more accessible. As incidents involving groups like DarkSide and PYSA increased, federal agencies like the FBI and CISA stressed the importance of user training, regular system updates, and maintaining offline backups to mitigate risks associated with these evolving threats. The trends observed in 2021 underscore the imperative for organizations to enhance their cybersecurity posture in response to the growing sophistication and frequency of cyberattacks.

## 2022
In 2022, the cyber fraud landscape was markedly influenced by a surge in ransomware attacks, particularly targeting sectors such as healthcare, public health, and agriculture. Criminal actors, including state-sponsored groups from North Korea and Iran, employed advanced tactics such as exploiting software vulnerabilities and conducting spearphishing campaigns to infiltrate networks and extort organizations. The rise of Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) models, exemplified by groups like LockBit 2.0, further complicated the threat landscape, as attackers utilized sophisticated methods to maximize their impact and pressure victims into compliance.

The increase in cyber incidents underscored the critical need for organizations to adopt comprehensive cybersecurity strategies. Recommendations included enhancing employee training on phishing awareness, implementing robust technical defenses such as anti-phishing measures and multifactor authentication, and maintaining regular updates and backups to mitigate the risks associated with evolving cyber threats. Overall, 2022 highlighted the importance of a proactive and collaborative approach to cybersecurity, emphasizing the necessity for organizations to fortify their defenses against increasingly sophisticated attacks.

## 2023
In 2023, the cybersecurity landscape experienced a significant surge in ransomware attacks, primarily driven by sophisticated groups such as ALPHV Blackcat, Play, and LockBit 3.0. These actors have refined their tactics, employing advanced methods including double extortion and the exploitation of known vulnerabilities to compromise critical infrastructure and various sectors, notably healthcare and education. The emergence of new threats, such as the BianLian and Rhysida ransomware groups, highlights the increasing complexity of the threat environment, particularly with their use of data exfiltration as a primary tactic. Furthermore, state-sponsored actors, particularly from North Korea and Russia, have targeted critical infrastructure, emphasizing the demand for enhanced cybersecurity measures across organizations.

To counteract these evolving threats, organizations are urged to implement robust security protocols, including phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication, timely patch management, and regular employee training on recognizing social engineering attempts. The Joint Ransomware Task Force has been instrumental in fostering collaboration between government and private sectors to develop best practices and enhance incident response capabilities. As ransomware tactics continue to evolve, maintaining a proactive defense posture and adhering to established cybersecurity frameworks remain paramount in safeguarding against these persistent and sophisticated cyber threats.

## 2024
As we transition into 2024, the cybersecurity landscape remains fraught with challenges, particularly from advanced threat actors such as the HiatusRAT and BianLian ransomware group. The HiatusRAT, a sophisticated Remote Access Trojan, has been increasingly utilized to compromise network infrastructures, highlighting the urgent need for organizations to adopt stringent cybersecurity measures, including robust monitoring and access controls. Furthermore, the BianLian group has evolved its tactics from double-extortion to exfiltration-based extortion, necessitating stronger defenses like phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication and effective network segmentation. The threat landscape is also marked by increased ransomware activities, particularly from state-sponsored groups linked to Iran and North Korea, employing advanced techniques such as living off the land and exploiting known vulnerabilities.

Organizations must remain vigilant and proactive in their cybersecurity strategies, focusing on comprehensive training, timely vulnerability management, and the implementation of structured frameworks like those developed by CISA and NIST. As cyber threats grow in sophistication, particularly through the use of ransomware variants and state-sponsored operations targeting critical infrastructure, enhancing security posture through continuous threat hunting and the integration of robust security measures will be essential. The ongoing collaboration among international cybersecurity agencies aims to bolster defenses against these evolving threats, ensuring the protection of sensitive data and the integrity of essential services.

## 2025
As we advance into 2025, the cybersecurity landscape surrounding Operational Technology (OT) has become increasingly complex and fraught with challenges. The sophistication of threat actors has risen significantly, with notable groups such as Scattered Spider and Iranian-affiliated cyber actors utilizing advanced social engineering tactics and ransomware variants to exploit vulnerabilities in critical infrastructure. In particular, the rise of ransomware employing double extortion tactics and the emergence of phishing-as-a-service platforms have exacerbated the threat environment. To counter these evolving threats, organizations must implement comprehensive risk management frameworks, prioritize asset inventory, and enhance cross-collaboration between IT and OT teams. 

The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) and other organizations emphasize the necessity of adopting robust cybersecurity measures, such as real-time monitoring, phishing-resistant multifactor authentication, and timely patching, to safeguard against these risks. Moreover, the ongoing collaboration between various cybersecurity agencies aims to bolster defenses and ensure that organizations remain vigilant against sophisticated attack vectors. As the landscape continues to evolve, it is critical for organizations to adopt a proactive approach in securing their OT systems and maintaining resilience against emerging cyber threats.


=== Overall Report ===

Between 2020 and 2025, the cybersecurity landscape has faced significant challenges due to the rise in sophisticated cyber threats, particularly from ransomware groups and state-sponsored actors. Notably, ransomware attacks have become increasingly prevalent, characterized by the adoption of double-extortion tactics, where attackers not only encrypt data but also threaten to publish sensitive information unless ransoms are paid. Groups such as LockBit, Akira, and BlackSuit have exploited vulnerabilities in critical infrastructure, including healthcare and public services, leading to severe operational disruptions and financial losses. The period has also seen a marked increase in phishing attacks, particularly spear-phishing, which continue to evolve in complexity as threat actors leverage social engineering techniques to infiltrate networks.

In response to these evolving threats, organizations have been urged to adopt robust cybersecurity measures, including phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication, timely software updates, and comprehensive incident response strategies. Furthermore, the emphasis on securing operational technology (OT) systems and implementing proactive threat modeling has become crucial to mitigating risks associated with advanced cyber adversaries. As the threat landscape continues to shift, a collaborative approach among government agencies and private sectors is essential to enhance overall cybersecurity resilience and protect sensitive data from persistent attacks.
